10 Mental Math Tricks That Make You Faster Than a Calculator

July 15, 2026 · 7 min read · Brain vs Math

By the time you've unlocked your phone, opened the calculator and typed the numbers, a practiced mental calculator has already said the answer out loud. The gap isn't talent — it's a small set of patterns that turn "hard" arithmetic into easy arithmetic. Here are the ten with the best effort-to-payoff ratio. Each takes under a minute to learn.

1. The 11s shortcut

To multiply a two-digit number by 11, add its digits and slot the sum in the middle.

52 × 11: 5 + 2 = 7 → slot it between 5 and 2 → 572. If the digits sum past 9, carry: 68 × 11 → 6+8 = 14 → 6(4)8 with a carry → 748.

2. Multiply by 5: halve, then ×10

Fives feel awkward, but halving rarely does. Cut the number in half, then move the decimal.

86 × 5: half of 86 is 43 → 430. Works in reverse for ÷5: double it, then ÷10 (94 ÷ 5 → 188 → 18.8).

3. Squaring numbers that end in 5

Take the tens digit, multiply it by one more than itself, then append 25.

75²: 7 × 8 = 56 → append 25 → 5625. Same for 35² → 3×4=12 → 1225.

4. The percentage flip

X% of Y always equals Y% of X. When one direction is ugly, flip it.

8% of 50 feels slow — but 50% of 8 is instantly 4. Same number, friendlier packaging.

5. Doubling and halving

When one factor is even, halve it and double the other. Repeat until the problem is easy.

16 × 35: halve 16, double 35 → 8 × 70 = 560. One step turned a two-digit multiplication into a times-table fact.

6. Add and subtract left to right

School teaches right-to-left with carrying — accurate on paper, slow in your head. Mentally, work big to small: hundreds, then tens, then ones.

487 + 245: 400+200 = 600 → 80+40 = 120 (running total 720) → 7+5 = 12 → 732. You always hold one tidy number, not a column of carries.

7. Subtract by rounding up

Subtracting near a round number? Overshoot to the round number, then give back the difference.

634 − 298: subtract 300 → 334, give back 2 → 336. Your brain does one clean jump instead of double borrowing.

8. The 9s pattern

Every fact in the 9 times table encodes itself: the tens digit is one less than the multiplier, and the digits sum to 9.

9 × 7: tens digit 6 (one less than 7), digits must sum to 9 → 63. Instant check for any 9-fact you half-remember.

9. Divisibility checks in one glance

  • ÷3? Digit sum divisible by 3 (running check for 741: 7+4+1 = 12 → yes).
  • ÷4? Last two digits divisible by 4 (…316 → 16 → yes).
  • ÷9? Digit sum divisible by 9.
  • ÷6? Even and passes the 3-check.

These turn "does it divide evenly?" from a guess into a two-second scan — exactly the skill our Daily Target puzzle rewards when you're deciding whether ÷ is a legal move.

10. Benchmark estimation

For real-world math, a fast answer within 2% beats a slow exact one. Round to friendly anchors, compute, then nudge.

23 × 19: anchor at 23 × 20 = 460, then subtract one 23 → 437. Restaurant math, unit prices, currency conversion — all live here.

Make it automatic (the 5-minute plan)

Reading tricks is entertainment; using them under light pressure is training. The routine that works:

  1. 2 minutes: one run of Math Sprint — the adaptive difficulty forces you to apply shortcuts, not just recall facts.
  2. 2 minutes: today's Daily Target Warm-up and Easy tiers — combining numbers strategically is where tricks 4, 5 and 9 shine.
